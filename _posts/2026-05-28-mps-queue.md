I visited my MP's Meet-the-People Session the other day. Walked in, joined a queue. Got to the front. They checked my IC, asked what my issue was about, and handed me a sticker with a queue number on it. I stuck it on my shirt and sat down.

Then I waited. A volunteer walked around the room looking at people's stickers until they found mine and called me up.

There was a queue to get a queue number. And a person whose job was to find a sticker on someone's shirt. This is how it works in 2026.

---

## The Process

Walk in. Queue. IC check to confirm you're from the constituency. Category triage. Queue number on a small sticker. Sit down. Wait for someone to spot your sticker. Get called up. Talk to a volunteer. Volunteer may redirect you to the MP.

About 30 people were there. The whole thing took about an hour. Not terrible for volume, but the process itself is where it falls apart.

Two problems stand out:

**1. The queue for the queue.** The first queue doesn't get you closer to the person helping you. It gets you into the waiting pool. You queue just to get permission to wait somewhere else. This is admin overhead that could be automated.

**2. The sticker hunt.** The queue number is printed on a small sticker stuck to your shirt. Volunteers have to physically scan the room, looking at each person's sticker until they find the next number. If your sticker is folded, angled, or you're facing another direction, they walk past you. Then they have to double back.

---

## The Fix

Replace the manual queue with a self-service kiosk:

1. Scan your NRIC barcode at the kiosk. The system checks the constituency database to confirm you're a resident.

2. The kiosk prints a ticket with a queue number and a QR code.

3. Scan the QR code on your phone. Fill in your category and briefly describe your issue. All done before you sit down.

4. When it's your turn, you get an SMS. No need to stay in the room watching the sticker-hunter. Go grab a drink, come back.

5. Scan the same QR code again anytime to see real-time wait estimates: "You are #4 of 8 waiting. About 12 minutes."

This frees up the two volunteers who were handling IC checks and triage. They can open another table to help residents faster. The people who can use the kiosk (probably 70-80% of visitors) self-serve. Elderly residents who come alone get one assisted lane. Most older folks come with someone younger anyway, so the volume on the assisted lane is small.

---

## Why It Hasn't Been Done

Nobody at MPS is complaining loudly because nobody expects better. It's always been like this, so it stays like this. Constituency offices aren't set up to buy kiosks or build forms. The whole system is invisible to anyone who doesn't attend, so there's no public pressure.

It's not about buying expensive hardware. A barcode scanner and a Raspberry Pi running a basic form would replace two manual steps. The hard part isn't the tech. It's someone deciding that a sticker-based queue system from the 1980s doesn't need to stay a sticker-based queue system in 2026.

---

The MP I met was helpful. The volunteers were kind. The process around them was the problem. You shouldn't need to queue twice just to queue once.
