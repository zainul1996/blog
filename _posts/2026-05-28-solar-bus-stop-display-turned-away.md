NCS's CEO [posted on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/samliew_sustainability-busarrival-transportation-share-7464185277130117120-v0vj) this week, standing proudly next to a new
solar-powered bus arrival display. He's smiling. The sun is out. The screen is
bright and readable. Problem is — if you're sitting inside the bus stop, the
screen is behind you.

Not metaphorically. Physically.

---

## What the Photo Shows

The display is mounted on a standalone pole in front of the shelter. The screen
is rotated roughly 90 degrees — facing outward toward the approach, away from
the people waiting under the roof. Someone sitting inside has the back of the
display unit in their peripheral vision. To read the bus timings, they need to
turn around completely, stand up, and step toward the pole.

The display itself isn't anything special — it's the same bus ETA board we
already have at some stops around Singapore. Just solar-powered. And worse
placed.

What makes this remarkable is that there are properly placed displays already
in service. Mounted under the shelter roof, facing commuters. You glance up
and the next bus is right there. Those work fine. NCS built a new version and
somehow managed to take a step backward.

---

## The Problem Isn't Just the Rain

Everyone's first reaction is "well, what if it's raining?" And yes — in a
country where it rains half the year, putting the only arrival display outside
the shelter is a special kind of oversight. But rain is just the amplifier.

The core issue is **orientation**. The display isn't designed for the person
already at the bus stop — it's designed for the person walking toward it. The
screen faces the approach path, so someone 20 metres away can see the arrival
time and decide to speed up. That's a useful secondary function, but it
shouldn't be the primary one.

The person who *needs* the bus timing most is the one already standing under
the shelter. They're the ones deciding whether to wait, grab a drink, or check
if they just missed the bus. Making them step out to check — rain or shine —
is a failure of placement disguised as innovation.

---

## Why This Happened

This is what "Smart Nation" looks like when it's optimised for announcement
photos instead of actual use.

A new pole, a new display, a clear shot for the press — that's easy to
photograph, easy to celebrate. Roof-integrated solar panels and a sheltered
display under the existing shelter? Harder to frame, harder to explain in a
LinkedIn post. Requires coordination with LTA, structural assessment,
custom mounting.

The existing ETA displays in Singapore prove it can be done right. They're
under the roof, facing inward, integrated into the shelter itself. NCS just
chose not to follow that pattern — and the CEO celebrated the result.

---

## The Fix

The fix is straightforward, and you don't need to reinvent the bus stop:

1. **Solar on the roof, not beside it.** Every bus stop already has a roof.
Line it with solar panels (building-integrated photovoltaics — BIPV). No extra
poles, no new footings, no additional concrete.

2. **Display under the shelter, facing commuters.** Mount it where people sit,
the same way the existing properly-placed displays work. The screen is
sheltered from sun and rain, lasts longer, and serves the person who needs it.

3. **A secondary outward-facing display for approach.** A smaller, high-
contrast screen on the outer edge of the shelter so approaching commuters can
see the arrival time from 20 metres away. Not the main display — a complement
to it.

This gives you every use case covered: the person waiting, the person
approaching, and zero exposure to Singapore's rain.

---

## Smart Nation Should Be Invisible

The best public infrastructure innovation is the kind you don't notice. You
shouldn't see the solar panels — you should just never wonder why the bus
timing screen works during a blackout. You shouldn't applaud the new ETA
display — you should just never need your phone to check when the next bus
arrives.

When the CEO is the only one smiling next to the display, you've designed for
the wrong audience.
